Chapter 01

Before the VedasIndia did not always have caste

To understand what was done to you, you first need to understand what was there before.

From approximately 3300 BCE to 1300 BCE — that is, more than 4,000 years ago — one of the greatest civilizations in human history existed in northwestern India and present-day Pakistan. We call it the Indus Valley Civilization (IVC), also known as the Harappan Civilization.

What the IVC looked like

The IVC cities of Mohenjo-daro and Harappa were marvels. They had planned streets in a grid pattern, which no other civilization of that time had. They had an advanced sewage and drainage system — London would not have a comparable system until the 1850s. They had standardized weights and measures for fair trade. Their houses had private bathrooms.

Most importantly: archaeologists have found no evidence of a rigid social hierarchy in IVC. There are no massive royal palaces towering over the poor. There are no mass graves of sacrificed slaves. The houses, while varying in size, do not show the extreme inequality we see in civilizations built on enforced hierarchy.

Archaeological Record

What IVC religion looked like — before caste

The famous "Pashupati Seal" shows a meditating figure surrounded by animals — possibly an early form of what becomes Shiva, but in a non-Brahmanical, non-hierarchical context. Mother goddess figurines are abundant. There is a Great Bath — suggesting ritual purification. But there are no fire altars of the Vedic type. There is no evidence of the animal sacrifice system that Vedic religion would later build its power on.

Source: Archaeological Survey of India; Shereen Ratnagar, "Understanding Harappa"

Then what happened?

Around 1500–1200 BCE, groups of people speaking an early form of Sanskrit arrived in northwestern India from Central Asia and the Pontic Steppe (the grasslands between the Black Sea and the Caspian Sea). Modern genetic and linguistic science has confirmed this. They were called Aryans — meaning "noble" in their own language.

They brought with them: a language (Sanskrit), a set of hymns (what would become the Rig Veda), a ritual system centered on fire sacrifice (yajna), a priestly class (Brahmins), and the early seeds of what would become the caste system.

"The caste system was not always here. India did not emerge from creation with four castes carved into it. It was built — deliberately, over centuries — by a small priestly class to secure permanent power for themselves." — Analysis based on the work of historians Romila Thapar, D.D. Kosambi, and B.R. Ambedkar

This is the first and most important thing to understand: Caste was not inevitable. It was invented. And what is invented by human beings can be dismantled by human beings.

Chapter 02

How the System Was BuiltThe architecture of permanent inequality

Imagine you want to create a system where your family and your friends' families will be at the top of society forever — not just this generation, but for thousands of years. What would you need?

You would need: (1) A story that makes the hierarchy seem natural or divine. (2) Laws that enforce it with violence. (3) Control of education so only you know the rules. (4) A spiritual mechanism that makes the oppressed accept their oppression.

This is exactly what was built. Let us examine each piece.

Piece 1: The Divine Story — Purusha Sukta

In the Rig Veda, one of the oldest texts in the world, there is a hymn called the Purusha Sukta (Rig Veda 10.90). It describes a cosmic being (Purusha) whose body is sacrificed to create the world — and society.

Primary Text — Rig Veda 10.90, verses 11–12

The Caste Origin Verse

Original Sanskrit (transliterated): "Brahmanosya mukham asit, bahu rajanyah kritah, uru tadasya yad vaishyah, padbhyam shudro ajayata."

Translation: "The Brahmin was his mouth. The Kshatriya was made from his arms. His thighs became the Vaishya. From his feet, the Shudra was born."

What this means in plain language: Your position in society is not the result of your choices, your intelligence, or your work. It is determined before you are born — by which part of God's body you supposedly came from. The Brahmin (priest) came from the holy mouth. The Shudra (laborer) came from the dirty feet. This is presented as cosmic, eternal, divine law.

The hidden politics: Who wrote this? The Brahmin priests wrote it. They placed themselves at the top — the "mouth of god." They declared this divine arrangement. They were not reporting a fact; they were manufacturing justification for their own dominance, and attributing it to god so it could not be questioned.

Source: Rig Veda 10.90.11-12 — accessible at sacred-texts.com/hin/rigveda

Piece 2: Control of Knowledge

The second pillar of the system was absolute monopoly on knowledge. The Vedas were kept in Sanskrit — a language that only Brahmin boys were taught. The rules of the system were written in Sanskrit. The laws were in Sanskrit. The arguments for why the system was fair were in Sanskrit. And the people most harmed by the system — Shudras, Dalits, women — were explicitly forbidden from learning Sanskrit.

Gautama Dharmashastra — Ancient Law Text

Punishment for a Shudra hearing the Vedas

The Gautama Dharmashastra (one of the ancient law codes) specifies: if a Shudra intentionally overheard Vedic recitation, molten tin was to be poured into his ears. If he repeated Vedic verses from memory, his tongue was to be cut out.

The long-term vision behind this: If you cannot read, you cannot know your rights. If you cannot know your rights, you cannot claim them. If the only people who know the law are the people who benefit from it, the law will never change. This is not incidental — it is the architecture of permanent power.

Source: Gautama Dharmashastra; confirmed in Ambedkar's "Annihilation of Caste"

Piece 3: The Karma Trap

Perhaps the most brilliant and most cruel invention was the karma doctrine as it was applied to caste. Here is how it worked:

You are a Dalit. You have no land. You cannot read. You are forced to handle dead bodies, clean sewage, remove animal carcasses — the most degrading tasks. Your children will do the same. You suffer every day.

The Brahmanical answer to your suffering: "This is your karma from a past life. You did something terrible in your previous birth. God is giving you this life as punishment. If you accept your suffering without complaint, serve those above you faithfully, you will be born into a higher caste next time."

"The karma doctrine, as applied to caste, is the most sophisticated oppression mechanism in human history. It removes the oppressor from all moral accountability. It places the burden of the crime on the victim. And it does so in the name of god." — Based on Ambedkar's analysis in "The Annihilation of Caste" (1936)

Notice what this does: It makes the victim responsible for their own oppression. The Brahmin landlord who exploits you is not a criminal — he is simply enjoying his good karma from past virtue. You, who are exploited, deserve it — because of your past-life sin. The system is presented as not only fair, but divinely just.

Jyotirao Phule (1827–1890), the great reformer from Maharashtra, was one of the first to name this mechanism clearly. He called it a system of religious fraud.

Piece 4: Control of Every Life Event

Once the Brahmin controlled the story, the knowledge, and the spiritual interpretation — they also controlled every moment of a person's life.

  • Birth: The naming ceremony (namakarana) required a Brahmin — for a fee.
  • Growing up: The thread ceremony (upanayana) was denied to Shudras entirely.
  • Marriage: Auspicious dates required a Brahmin to calculate — for a fee.
  • Farming: When to sow, when to harvest — required Brahmin astrological approval — for a fee.
  • Death: Funeral rites required a Brahmin to perform — or the soul would not reach heaven — for a fee.
  • After death: The Shradh (ancestor rites) required a Brahmin — annually — for a fee.

The Brahmin was the mandatory middleman at every critical moment of life. There was no way around him. And he was always paid. This was not incidental — it was a business model wearing the costume of religion.

Chapter 03

It was also the text that British colonial administrator Warren Hastings had selected in 1772 as "the Hindu law code" — making it the official basis for administering law to Hindus under the British Empire. This gave it even more authority than it historically had.

Important Clarification

Is Manusmriti still in use today?

After independence, Ambedkar ensured the Indian Constitution guaranteed equality regardless of caste, religion, or gender. Manusmriti is not legally applicable in modern India. However, its cultural and social effects persist — in honor killings, in inter-caste marriage violence, in the daily humiliations still faced by Dalit communities. The law changed. The mindset is still being dismantled.

The Manusmriti was burned publicly on December 25, 1927, by Ambedkar and his followers at the Mahad Satyagraha — one of the most powerful symbolic acts in Indian history.

Chapter 04

What Was Done to WomenThe other half of the oppression

The caste system and the patriarchal system were not separate. They were two faces of the same coin. A Brahmin woman suffered less than a Dalit woman, but both suffered — in different ways, at different hands. Here is what the texts actually prescribed for women, and what was done in their name.

The Core Doctrine: Women Cannot Be Independent

Manusmriti 5.147–149 — The Foundation

Permanent Guardianship

The text states clearly: A woman must never be independent. In childhood she is under her father. In youth, under her husband. In old age, under her son. She must never be granted independence. Her husband, even if he is morally degraded, even if he lacks good qualities, even if he has no virtues — must be served as a god by a virtuous woman.

What this means in practice: No property. No legal standing. No right to refuse marriage. No right to remarry after a husband's death. No right to leave a violent marriage. All of this was divinely sanctioned.

Source: Manusmriti 5.147–149 — Bühler translation

The Rig Veda's Declaration About Women's Intelligence

Rig Veda 8.33.17 — Original, Pre-Buddhist text

Indra declares women intellectually inferior

The god Indra — King of the Gods — is quoted in the Rig Veda as saying: "The mind of woman cannot be disciplined; she has very little intelligence."

Who is speaking? Not a human critic. Not a misguided philosopher. The king of the gods — the most powerful divine being in the Vedic universe — is quoted as making this declaration. This is presented as divine testimony, not human opinion.

The political purpose: If god himself says women are not intelligent, then denying women education is not oppression — it is simply following the divine order of things. The declaration manufactures justification for a system that benefits from women's exclusion from knowledge.

Source: Rig Veda 8.33.17 — sacred-texts.com/hin/rigveda

Sita — The Model Woman and What It Demanded

The Ramayana is perhaps the most widely known story in India. It presents Sita as the ideal woman. Let us look at what that ideal actually required of real women.

Valmiki Ramayana — Bala Kanda & Uttara Kanda

What happened to Sita

Sita was abducted — she was a victim of kidnapping. After Rama rescues her, she is made to walk through fire (Agni Pariksha) to prove she was not "used" by Ravana. She passes this test. The fire itself declares her pure. Rama accepts her back.

Then — after they return to Ayodhya, after she is his queen, after she has passed the divine fire test — Rama hears a washerman questioning Sita's purity. Rama does not defend his wife. He does not cite the fire test. He abandons a pregnant Sita in the forest because of a washerman's gossip.

The message to real women: Even if you pass god's own test. Even if you are completely innocent. Even if a divine fire declared you pure. One man's doubt about your body is enough to destroy your life. A woman's honor is never her own — it is always held by men's perception of her.

Source: Valmiki Ramayana, Uttara Kanda, Ch. 40-48; critical edition, Baroda Oriental Institute

Draupadi — What the "Righteous" Heroes Did

Mahabharata — Sabha Parva, Chapters 67–72

A wife gambled away by her "Dharmaraj" husband

Yudhishthira — called Dharmaraj (King of Righteousness) — loses everything in a dice game. When he has nothing left, he wagers his wife Draupadi. He loses her too. She is then summoned to the court and dragged by her hair in front of the assembly. An attempt is made to disrobe her publicly.

In the hall were: Bhishma (the grandsire, the most respected elder), Drona (the master teacher), Kripa (the royal priest), and dozens of other warriors and kings. All of them sat in silence.

Draupadi's question — which nobody answered: "Was I wagered after my lord had lost himself, or before? If he had already lost himself, did he have any right to wager me at all?"

It is one of the most powerful legal arguments in all ancient literature. And it was asked by a woman. And no one in the hall could answer it. Not one of the great "righteous" men stood up.

The same Yudhishthira is celebrated throughout the epic as the embodiment of dharma. This is the dharma.

Source: Mahabharata, Sabha Parva, Chapters 67–72 — Ganguli translation, sacred-texts.com

The Devadasi System — Religious Prostitution

The Devadasi system was the institutionalization of sexual exploitation with full religious sanction. Here is how it worked:

Young girls — predominantly from Dalit and lower-caste communities — were "married" to the temple deity between the ages of 8 and 14. Once married to the god, they could never marry a human man. They were then made sexually available to Brahmin priests and upper-caste patrons as an act of religious devotion.

The girl was told she was blessed. Her family was told she was serving god. The priests and patrons who used her were told they were receiving divine merit. The children she bore had no legal father, no caste protection, no social standing.

This was not fringe practice. It operated across South India — in Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Maharashtra — for centuries, with full Brahmanical scriptural backing. The British colonial government formally prohibited it in 1934. The practice continued in many areas for decades after.

"The devadasi was not a prostitute who happened to be at a temple. She was a person whose entire identity — her body, her labor, her children — was systematically owned by a religious and social institution that refused to even acknowledge the ownership." — Based on Gail Omvedt's research in "Buddhism in India: Challenging Brahmanism and Caste"
Chapter 05

The Making of UntouchabilityHow a people were made into walking pollution

The concept of "untouchability" is one of the most extreme forms of social exclusion ever systematically practiced in human history. To understand it, we must ask: Where did it come from? Was it always there?

Ambedkar's answer — backed by his historical research — is devastating: Untouchability was not ancient. It was created as a punishment.

Ambedkar's Theory: Untouchability and Buddhism

Ambedkar argued in his book "The Untouchables" (1948) that the people who became Untouchables were originally Buddhist. When the Brahmanical revival pushed Buddhism out of India between 400–800 CE, most people returned to the caste fold. But some refused. They held on to Buddhist practices — including the Buddhist prohibition on killing animals.

Because they refused to kill cows (following Buddhist ahimsa), they only handled cattle that had died naturally. The upper-caste communities then made this into a stigma — calling them "polluted" for touching dead animals. The very act of following a compassionate Buddhist principle was weaponized against them.

Ambedkar, "The Untouchables" (1948)

The broken men hypothesis

Ambedkar identified that Untouchables typically lived in settlements just outside the village — not inside. He traced this pattern to Buddhist monastery placements, which were also traditionally outside villages. He found that Untouchable communities' traditional occupations (handling dead cattle, working with leather, cleaning sewage) all correspond to occupations that arose specifically from the refusal to kill animals. His conclusion: The people called Untouchables were the remnant Buddhist population who refused to re-enter the caste system after the Brahmanical revival — and were punished for it by being made into "walking pollution."

Source: B.R. Ambedkar, "The Untouchables: Who Were They and Why They Became Untouchables?" (1948)

What Untouchability Meant in Daily Life

This was not an abstract legal category. It shaped every moment of every day. From Manusmriti 10.51–56 and confirmed by Chinese traveller Xuanzang (7th century CE) who personally witnessed it:

Historical Record — Multiple Sources

The daily reality of untouchability

Untouchable communities were required to: Live in settlements outside the village. Use only broken pots. Wear clothes taken from the dead. Wear iron ornaments (not gold or silver). Be prohibited from wearing shoes in the presence of upper-caste persons. In some regions: ring a bell or clap while walking so upper castes could avoid their shadow falling on them. Their shadow falling on food or a person required ritual purification. They could not use public wells. They could not enter temples. They could not eat in the same space as upper castes.

This was not the practice of isolated extremists. It was prescribed in the sacred law texts and enforced by the community across centuries and regions. Xuanzang, the Chinese Buddhist monk who traveled India in 629–645 CE, documented Untouchable communities being made to announce their presence as they moved through towns.

Sources: Manusmriti 10.51-56; Xuanzang's "Great Tang Records on the Western Regions" (7th c. CE); Al-Biruni's "Kitab ul Hind" (1030 CE)

The Three Independent Witnesses

Those who claim caste oppression is exaggerated or foreign-imposed must explain three independent non-Brahmanical witnesses from different centuries:

~500 BCE

The Pali Buddhist Canon

Written in Pali (not Sanskrit, not Brahmin-controlled), the Buddhist texts extensively document and critique the Brahmin caste system as a social reality. This is an independent tradition that had no motive to fabricate caste oppression.

629–645 CE

Xuanzang — Chinese Buddhist Monk

Foreign observer with no stake in Indian politics. His detailed travel accounts document untouchability as a lived reality he personally witnessed across multiple regions of India.

1030 CE

Al-Biruni — Arab Scholar

The greatest scholar of the medieval Islamic world. In his "Kitab ul Hind," he provides precise sociological documentation of the caste system — centuries before any British colonial intervention. He describes the social separation with the detachment of an anthropologist.

Three independent sources. Three different languages. Three different centuries. Three different cultural perspectives. All describing the same system. This is not propaganda — it is documented history.

Chapter 06

The Sacred Texts — What They Actually SayOriginal and manipulated — and why it matters

When you question the caste system using scriptural evidence, defenders often say: "Those bad parts were added later by enemies. The real Vedas are pure."

Here is the honest, evidence-based answer to that claim.

What is provably original and already problematic

The Charvakas were India's own ancient atheist-materialist tradition. Around 600 BCE — before any Muslim, Christian, or foreign rule — they wrote a critique of Vedic rituals. Their critique is preserved in the Sarva Darsana Samgraha. They called the authors of the Ashvamedha (a Vedic ritual with explicit sexual components) "buffoons and demons." They were mocking their own tradition's texts.

This single fact is the most powerful evidence: Indian people were critiquing these texts from inside India, in ancient India, before any foreign ruler arrived. The problems in the original texts are not foreign insertions — they were noticed and criticized by Indians themselves 2,600 years ago.

The Most Important Conclusion

The interpolation argument — that all the bad parts were added by enemies — is a deflection. Here is why it does not work:

Even if you removed every single later addition. Even if you cleaned the Puranas of every medieval insertion. You would still be left with: the Rig Veda's caste verse, Manusmriti's torture laws, the Brihadaranyaka's forced sex passage, the Ashvamedha, and the karma-justification of social oppression. All of these predate Muslim rule, Christian rule, and British rule by centuries or millennia.

The oppression is not in the corruptions. The oppression is in the originals.

Chapter 07

Why Buddhism Was BornThe greatest anti-caste revolution in history

To understand why Buddhism arose, you need to understand the world it arose in.

It was approximately 500 BCE. The Vedic Brahmanical system had expanded from northwestern India into the Gangetic Plains. Elaborate fire sacrifices (yajnas) required enormous wealth — thousands of cattle killed, enormous resources burned, all paid for by kings and received by Brahmin priests. The caste system was hardening. Women were being pushed further from public life. The Shudras were told their function was to serve. And anyone who questioned this was told they were questioning god's own design.

Where Buddha came from — and what his father followed

Siddhartha Gautama was born in Kapilavastu — in the foothills of the Himalayas, in what is today southern Nepal. His father was Suddhodana, the leader (raja) of the Shakya clan.

The Shakya clan lived in the Greater Magadha cultural region — an area that modern historians confirm was largely outside the reach of Vedic Brahminism at that time. The Shakyas followed their own tribal-republican customs. They had their own council (gana sangha). They practiced their own forms of nature worship and ancestral customs — not the Brahmin-mediated Vedic rituals. Suddhodana was not a Vedic Hindu. After his son became the Buddha and returned to teach him, Suddhodana heard the teachings and converted to Buddhism, becoming, according to Buddhist tradition, an arahant (one who has achieved liberation) before his death.

What Buddha directly challenged

Buddha's Direct Teachings — Pali Canon

The core anti-caste declarations

The Buddha directly attacked the logic of birth-based hierarchy in multiple suttas. In the Vasala Sutta (Sutta Nipata 1.7), he declared: A person does not become a brahmin by birth. A person does not become an outcast by birth. It is one's actions that make one a brahmin. It is one's actions that make one an outcast.

In the Assalayana Sutta (Majjhima Nikaya 93), when a young brahmin argued for caste superiority, the Buddha dismantled the argument point by point, asking: Do brahmins not come from the womb of women just as other people do? Do they not bleed if cut? Where exactly is this superiority that birth is supposed to confer?

His sangha (community) was open to all castes — including people who would have been considered untouchable under Brahmanical law. Sunita, a scavenger who cleaned streets, became a respected monk. Upali, a barber — the lowest of the low — became one of the most respected disciples.

Sources: Pali Canon — accessible at suttacentral.net (free, fully translated)

The most important thing Buddhism did

Buddhism did something that had never been done in India before at scale: it established institutions of knowledge that were not controlled by Brahmins. The Buddhist monasteries were schools. The Pali language — in which the Buddha's teachings were recorded — was not Sanskrit. It was the language of ordinary people. Anyone could learn it. Anyone could read the texts. Anyone could become a monk or a nun and enter the educated class.

This is why Nalanda — the great Buddhist university — housed an estimated 9 million manuscripts and 10,000 students at its peak. It was the largest institution of learning in the ancient world. And it was not restricted by birth.

The existence of Nalanda was itself a political act against the Brahmanical monopoly on knowledge.

Chapter 08

How Buddhism Was ErasedThe counter-revolution — and what it cost India

For nearly a thousand years, Buddhism was the dominant moral and intellectual tradition of the Indian subcontinent. The greatest empire India ever produced — the Mauryan Empire under Emperor Ashoka (268–232 BCE) — was Buddhist. Ashoka's rock edicts, carved in stone across the continent, promoted compassion, equality, and non-violence. They are the most tamper-proof evidence of this period — no one can claim they were "inserted later."

Then Buddhism declined in India — almost completely disappearing from the land of its birth by 1200 CE. How? This is one of the most important questions in Indian history, and one of the least honestly answered.

The Brahmanical Counter-Revolution — Step by Step

~200 BCE – 300 CE

The Bhakti strategy — absorbing the opposition

Brahmanical thinkers began incorporating elements of popular devotional religion (bhakti) that Buddhism had pioneered — accessible worship, emotional devotion, compassionate god-figures. This made Brahminism more appealing to common people who had been drawn to Buddhism's accessibility.

~300–600 CE

Buddha becomes Vishnu's avatar — the ultimate absorption

In the Bhagavata Purana and other texts, the Buddha is declared to be the 9th avatar of Vishnu. This seems like an honor. It is actually a hostile takeover. In these texts, Buddha is described as a deceiver — sent by Vishnu specifically to mislead the demons with false teachings. Buddhism is reframed as a divine trap, and its followers as people who fell for it. This is Brahmanical counter-propaganda at its most sophisticated.

~600–1000 CE

Adi Shankaracharya's philosophical counter-attack

Shankaracharya (788–820 CE) was a brilliant philosopher who traveled across India debating and defeating Buddhist scholars in public forums. Where Buddhism had provided philosophical frameworks that threatened Brahmanical authority, he built a counter-philosophy (Advaita Vedanta) that was intellectually formidable. With royal Brahmin patronage behind him and Buddhist institutions losing state support, this was devastating.

1193 CE

Nalanda destroyed

Bakhtiyar Khilji's army burned Nalanda. This is real and cannot be minimized — it was a catastrophic act of destruction. But its context matters: Nalanda's decline had already begun because Brahmanical kings had withdrawn patronage and funding. The fire finished what decades of institutional starvation had started. The manuscripts burned because there was no longer an institution strong enough to protect them.

1200–1400 CE

The punishment of those who refused to return

As Buddhism disappeared, communities that had converted returned to the caste fold — but were placed back, often in lower positions. The communities that refused to return — that held on to Buddhist egalitarian principles — were made into Untouchables. Ambedkar's research suggests this is the origin of untouchability as a social category. The people at the very bottom of the caste system are there because they were the ones who refused to accept the system.

"Untouchability is the symptom on the body of Hinduism of the disease called Buddhism." — B.R. Ambedkar, "The Untouchables: Who Were They?" (1948)

What India lost

When Buddhism was suppressed in India, India lost: its largest network of free educational institutions. Its most complete tradition of rational inquiry and logic. Its most powerful articulation of human equality. And the communities that most depended on these institutions — the Shudras, the women, the oppressed castes — lost their primary alternative to the Brahmanical system.

It took until 1956 for those communities to have a leader powerful enough to publicly name this and choose the path of return.

Chapter 09

The Ones Who Fought Back2,500 years of resistance — your real heritage

This history is not only a history of oppression. It is also — always, in every century — a history of resistance. Here are the people who refused. They are your real intellectual ancestors.

Gautama Buddha
563–483 BCE · Shakya Clan · Kapilavastu

Rejected caste, rejected Brahmin mediation, rejected animal sacrifice. Built the world's first open educational institution network. Taught that liberation is available to every human being regardless of birth.

"By birth one is not a brahmin nor an outcast. It is by deeds that one becomes a brahmin, and by deeds one becomes an outcast."

Charvakas
~600 BCE · India's Ancient Atheists

India's own rationalist-materialist tradition. Rejected the Vedas, rejected divine authority, rejected the karma doctrine. Called the Ashvamedha ritual authors "buffoons and demons" — in writing, in Sanskrit, in ancient India. Proof that Indian people questioned this from the very beginning.

"Heaven is merely a lie. The Vedas are the idle prattle of knaves."

Kabir
1440–1518 CE · Weaver · Varanasi

Born into a family of weavers (Muslim by faith, possibly Dalit-origin by community). Attacked both Hindu caste hierarchy and Islamic orthodoxy with equal courage. His poetry is still sung. Never learned Sanskrit. Wrote in the language of working people.

"If god is found by bathing in water, the frog living in the water must have found god first. If god is found in the forest, deer would be gods. If god is found in stone idols, I shall worship the grinding stone."

Tukaram
1608–1650 CE · Shudra Trader · Dehu, Maharashtra

A Shudra from Maharashtra who wrote abhangas (devotional poetry) that attacked Brahmin hypocrisy directly. Brahmin authorities demanded he destroy his writings. He refused. His abhangas survive and are sung across Maharashtra today.

"The knowledge of the Vedas is the birthright of all. Why do you guard it like a private property?"

Jyotirao Phule
1827–1890 CE · Mali Caste · Pune, Maharashtra

The father of the modern Indian anti-caste movement. In 1848, opened the first school for girls and Dalits in India — in his own house. His wife Savitribai became India's first female teacher. Named the Brahmanical system as deliberate fraud. Called for mass education as liberation. Ambedkar called him his guru.

"Education is the milk of a lioness — whoever drinks it becomes a lion."

Savitribai Phule
1831–1897 CE · India's First Female Teacher

Wife and intellectual partner of Jyotirao. When she walked to school to teach Dalit and low-caste children, upper-caste people threw stones and dung at her. She carried an extra sari in her bag and changed when she arrived. She kept walking. She never stopped. She is the mother of Indian public education.

"Go, get education. Be self-reliant, be industrious. Work, gather wisdom and riches, all gets lost without knowledge."

Periyar E.V. Ramasamy
1879–1973 CE · Self-Respect Movement · Tamil Nadu

The most radical anti-Brahmin thinker in South Indian history. Founded the Self-Respect Movement in 1925. Declared that god, religion, and the Congress party were all tools of Brahmin domination. Burned images of Rama and Manu publicly. Promoted widow remarriage, inter-caste marriage, and women's education as political acts.

"There is no god. There is no god. There is no god at all. He who invented god is a fool. He who propagates god is a scoundrel. He who worships god is a barbarian."

Savitribai & Fatima Sheikh
1848 CE · Partners in Education · Pune

Fatima Sheikh — India's first Muslim female teacher — opened her home and joined Savitribai Phule to teach the first school for Dalit and oppressed-caste girls in 1848. A Hindu Shudra woman and a Muslim woman, together, defying the entire social order. This story was erased from Indian school textbooks for over a century.

Their action was their statement: every child, regardless of caste or religion or gender, deserves to learn.

Chapter 10

Ambedkar's ChoiceWhy the greatest Indian jurist left Hinduism — and chose the Buddha

Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar was born on April 14, 1891, in Mhow, Central India, into the Mahar community — a Dalit community of Maharashtra. He would go on to earn degrees from Bombay University, Columbia University (New York), the London School of Economics, and Gray's Inn (London). He held more advanced degrees than almost any Indian of his generation.

And throughout all of this, he was treated as untouchable.

What he experienced — personally

As a child, he was made to sit on a gunny sack in school — separate from other children — because the teacher would not allow him on the benches. The class peon (who cleaned the school) refused to touch his water pot to fill it — so if no upper-caste student happened to help, he went without water all day.

As a young official working for the Baroda state, he could not find a place to stay in the city. No hotel would take him because of his caste. He disguised himself as a Parsi (a religious minority not subject to untouchability) and stayed in a Parsi inn — until he was discovered and physically thrown out.

As a law student in London, he was the most academically accomplished Indian there. When he returned to India, he could not find a house to rent in Bombay in a "good" area. Landlords who had accepted his application by post withdrew the moment they learned his surname.

His entire life was the proof of his argument. The man with the most degrees in the room was told he was ritually impure by birth. The man who wrote India's Constitution was told his shadow was polluting.

"I was born a Hindu. I had no choice in that. But I will not die a Hindu. Because to be a Hindu is to consent to a system that treats human beings as less than human." — B.R. Ambedkar, 1935 (after declaring his intention to leave Hinduism)

Why he chose Buddhism — specifically

After announcing in 1935 that he would leave Hinduism, Ambedkar spent 21 years studying all major religious traditions. He examined Islam, Christianity, Sikhism. He chose Buddhism. Here is why — in his own reasoning:

Ambedkar's Reasoning — "The Buddha and His Dhamma" (1956)

Why Buddhism and not another religion

On Islam: It offered equality within the community but maintained a sharp inside/outside division. The conversion to Islam had not liberated Dalit converts from caste prejudice in Muslim communities, either. It was also a foreign tradition with no roots in Indian soil or struggle.

On Christianity: Same problem — converts faced continued caste discrimination within Christian communities. And it was the religion of the colonial power that had oppressed India in a different way.

On Sikhism: Closer — Sikhism's founders explicitly rejected caste. But the tradition had narrowed significantly, and Dalit Sikhs were still facing discrimination in Sikh institutions.

On Buddhism: It was born in India. Its founder was Indian. It had already been the tradition of the oppressed classes for centuries. It rejected caste at its philosophical root — not as a social reform, but as a logical consequence of its core teaching that human beings have no inherent, fixed, birth-determined nature. It required no mediating priest. It could be practiced by anyone. And critically: the Dalits who converted would be returning to a tradition that Brahmanical power had taken from their ancestors — not adopting something foreign.

Source: B.R. Ambedkar, "The Buddha and His Dhamma" (1956); "Why I am not a Hindu" speeches (1935–1956)

October 14, 1956 — The Conversion

On October 14, 1956, in Nagpur, Maharashtra, Ambedkar publicly converted to Buddhism. He was accompanied by approximately 600,000 followers — the largest mass religious conversion in modern history, accomplished entirely peacefully, by a free choice of free people.

He recited the 22 vows he had composed — a direct repudiation of Brahmanical religion, vow by vow. They included: "I shall not believe that Brahma, Vishnu and Mahesh are gods." "I shall not perform Shraddha nor shall I give Pind-dan." "I shall not perform any rituals under the guidance of Brahmin priests." "I renounce Hinduism which is harmful for humanity."

He died six weeks later, on December 6, 1956. He had spent those six weeks completing "The Buddha and His Dhamma" — his life's final work.

What he gave India

Beyond his conversion, Ambedkar gave India its most important legal document: the Indian Constitution. As the Chairman of the Drafting Committee, he inserted Article 17 — which abolished untouchability and made its practice a criminal offence. He inserted affirmative action provisions. He inserted equality before law regardless of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth.

The man who had been denied water as a child because of his caste wrote the law that declared his oppression illegal.

What You Can DoThe tradition of rational thinking is yours to continue

You have now read what was done, who did it, why it was done, and who fought back. This final section is about what this knowledge is for — and what comes next.

"Educate. Agitate. Organize." — B.R. Ambedkar's three-word manifesto for liberation
01

Read the primary sources yourself

Don't take anyone's word for what the texts say — ours included. The Pali Canon is free at suttacentral.net. Ambedkar's complete works are free at ambedkar.org. The Manusmriti translation is at sacred-texts.com. Read them yourself.

02

Separate spirituality from the system

You can have your own relationship with the divine, with ethics, with the sacred — without accepting a system that tells you your birth determines your worth. These are not the same thing, and the conflation of the two was deliberate and manufactured.

03

Teach your children the real history

Phule, Periyar, Ambedkar, Savitribai, Kabir, Tukaram — these are the people who fought for your dignity. Make sure the children in your life know their names and their stories.

04

Practice inter-caste humanity

The system perpetuates itself when we enforce it in small, daily acts — in who we eat with, who we marry, how we speak about other communities. Every act of genuine human equality is a political act against the system.

05

Ask who benefits

When any religious or social rule is presented to you as eternal, divine, or natural — ask: who benefits from this rule? Who profits when I obey it? Who wrote it? These three questions will cut through most manufactured justifications.

06

Use your legal rights

Untouchability is a crime under Article 17 of the Indian Constitution. The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989 provides specific legal protection. Know your rights. Report violations. Organize with others.
